— Origin

Named for the weave.

Aya-ori — the diagonal interlocking weave of Heian court silk — names this building. The structure itself performs the same logic: each room offset from the next, each threshold a deliberate reorientation. You arrive, and the house has already begun its introduction.

The building dates from the early Meiji period. It was a machiya on a street designated — and legally protected — since 1640. When restoration began, the decision was never what to modernise, but what to leave undisturbed.

Established 1640 · Restored 2023 · Shimabara, Kyoto

— Values

What we believe.

Precision over comfort

A room arranged with exactness offers more rest than one arranged for ease. We trust the classical proportions.

The fact, not the adjective

"1640." "Ōmon-dōri." "Six rooms." These carry more weight than any description of luxury.

The unhurried introduction

Every culture of formal hospitality is a culture of introductions. We take the long version. There is time.

— 綾錦の精神

"The address already carries the history. Our work is to be precise enough that guests can feel it — and unhurried enough that they want to."

— History

The address in time.

1640
Shimabara designated

The Tokugawa shogunate legally establishes Shimabara as Kyoto's designated quarter. Its boundaries, and the address of this building, are fixed.

1880s
Machiya constructed

The current building is constructed in the Meiji period — a machiya whose proportions faithfully follow the classical Shimabara typology.

2023
Ayanishiki opens

After three years of careful restoration, the house opens as a six-room inn. The Ōmon gate remains. The street outside is still Shimabara.

Plum Room

— About This Room

Private engawa. Cypress bath. One scroll.

The Plum Room occupies the ground floor east wing, with a private engawa that opens directly onto the inner courtyard. The garden is six tatami mats of raked gravel — maintained each morning before breakfast. The sound of the rake is part of the stay.

A tokonoma alcove holds a single hanging scroll, changed monthly with the season. The hinoki cypress bath is entirely private — reserved for your room each evening, filled at the temperature you specify. The room accommodates two in futon, or up to four with additional bedding arranged on request.

茶の湯

Cha-no-yu · The Tea Ceremony

Performed by a practitioner who has made this movement forty thousand times. You sit in the designated position. The rest is already arranged.

— About This Experience

The form that remains.

The ceremony is conducted in the tea room on the ground floor — eight tatami mats, classical proportions — with a recessed hearth for autumn and winter, a portable brazier for spring and summer. The ceremony changes with the season. The form does not.

Our practitioner, who trained for twelve years in the Urasenke school, will guide you through the necessary movements — but not over-explain them. The ceremony is not a class. It is an encounter.

"The guest must master two things only: how to receive the bowl, and how to return it. Everything else is already decided."

Duration is approximately sixty minutes. There is no additional charge for hotel guests — it is part of your stay.

The Hinoki Bath

— About This Facility

Cypress, water, and time.

The bath is cut from Yoshino hinoki — the same timber used in shrine construction for over a thousand years. It was fitted in 2023 by a joiner who has never used a power tool. The scent is immediate.

Reserved per room, per session — never shared. The water temperature is set according to the season: 41°C in winter, 39°C in summer. Guests are asked to arrive without fragrance.

The last tayū house and the women who kept its form

An account of the six remaining tayū performers in Shimabara, their training from the age of nine, and the single ochaya that still stages a formal ozashiki.

By Ayanishiki Journal · March 18, 2025 · 6 min read

There are six of them left. The number itself is the first fact worth stating. Not because scarcity is the point, but because knowing six have continued — for how long, through what, out of what refusal or insistence — is more interesting than anything that can be said about their robes.

The training begins at nine. It ends when the practitioner decides it has ended, which is almost never. The tayū — a title, not a rank — learns seventeen arts. Dance and calligraphy are two. The others are less easily named in English. One of them is called the art of the introduction.

"Every culture of formal hospitality is a culture of introductions. The tayū learned this long before the word 'hospitality' existed in Kyoto dialect."

We met three of them in February, in a room that is also a record. The tatami mats are original — pre-Meiji, by the estimation of the shrine carpenter who replaced the skirting two years ago. The room is not shown to visitors. We were guests, not visitors, which requires a distinction we could not have made ourselves.

The Ōmon-dōri question

The question we had come to ask — whether the practices of the quarter could continue without the structure of the quarter — was not a question that could be answered directly. We were offered tea instead. This was itself a kind of answer.

What the quarter preserves is not the performance. It is the context that makes the performance legible. The gate is not a monument to what has passed. It is the boundary that makes the inside different from the outside. As long as the gate stands, Ōmon-dōri is not simply a street in Shimogyo Ward.

We left before the ceremony was complete. This is the correct behaviour. The tayū does not see you out. The gate does. You step through it, and Shimogyo Ward resumes. The transition is immediate, and it is still, after 385 years, absolutely precise.

— FAQ · よくある質問

Questions, answered plainly.

No ambiguity about what the stay includes. Ask anything that remains unclear.

Stay · 滞在
The room rate includes two meals — a traditional Japanese breakfast and a multi-course dinner. All in-house experiences (tea ceremony, ikebana, calligraphy) are also included. External activities and transport are not.
Check-in is from 15:00. Check-out is by 11:00. Early check-in and late check-out can sometimes be arranged — contact us directly before your arrival.
We welcome guests aged 12 and above. The house and its routines are designed for unhurried adults. We are glad to recommend nearby hotels better suited to families with younger children.
We have a two-night minimum. For the 離れ (Detached House), the minimum is three nights. We have found that one night is not enough to settle into the house properly.
Rooms · 客室
Three rooms have entirely private bathroom facilities. Two rooms share a dedicated cedar bath on the same floor — reserved per stay and never shared simultaneously. The 離れ has its own ceremonial bath.
Yes. The cooling and heating systems are fitted discreetly within the existing architecture. The temperature is managed by the house rather than by individual room controls, within a range we consider appropriate to the season.
Reservations · 予約
Cherry blossom season (late March–early April) and autumn foliage season (mid-November) book out four to six months in advance. For other periods, three to eight weeks is usually sufficient.
Cancellations more than 30 days before arrival incur no charge. Between 14 and 30 days: 30% of the total stay. Within 14 days: 100%. We recommend travel insurance for bookings made far in advance.
Access · アクセス
By taxi: approximately 10 minutes, ¥1,200–1,500. By bus: Kyoto City Bus route 207 to Shichijo-Karasuma, then 8 minutes on foot. We can arrange private transfer from any Kyoto station — ask at time of booking.
Still have questions?

We prefer a telephone call, but respond to all written enquiries within one business day.
